SECTION 1: TITLE OF THE INVENTION

"AEGIS-ZONE: A Zero-Trust Physical Workstation Security System Utilizing Continuous Biometric Variance and Spatial Wireless Intrusion Detection for Real-Time Cryptographic Key Derivation and Autonomous Purging"


SECTION 2: FIELD AND AREA OF INVENTION

The present invention pertains to the interdisciplinary convergence of Physical Security Engineering, Applied Cryptography, Internet of Things (IoT) Sensor Networks, and Zero-Trust Architecture. Specifically, this invention addresses the domain of continuous, real-time endpoint security at physically sensitive workstations by integrating distributed embedded hardware sensor arrays with autonomous cryptographic session management. The invention operates at the intersection of three technical disciplines: first, Biometric Signal Processing, namely the continuous acquisition and utilisation of physiological photoplethysmographic (PPG) signals from a user's peripheral vasculature as a source of high-entropy biological noise for cryptographic key material; second, Spatial Radio Frequency (RF) Analysis, employing distributed 2.4 GHz electromagnetic spectrum monitoring nodes to detect, classify, and localise wireless emitters within the immediate physical perimeter of a secured workstation; and third, Real-Time Symmetric Cryptography, applying SHA-3 (Keccak-256) hash-based key derivation and AES-256-CTR stream cipher operations governed entirely by the continuous state of physical environmental sensors rather than conventional static authentication handshakes.


SECTION 3: PRIOR PATENTS AND PUBLICATIONS FROM LITERATURE

A comprehensive review of pertinent prior art has been conducted to contextualise the present invention within the existing body of knowledge.

The first category of relevant prior art concerns point-in-time biometric authentication tokens. Standards such as the FIDO Alliance's FIDO2/WebAuthn specification (v2.3, ratified January 2026) and commercial fingerprint authentication modules employ discrete biometric verification at the moment of session initiation. Once the initial authentication handshake completes, the session persists indefinitely under an assumption of continuous trust. This approach is fundamentally vulnerable to "walk-away" session hijacking, wherein the authenticated user departs the workstation without explicitly terminating the session, and a subsequent actor inherits their full privileges.

The second category addresses cardiac and electrocardiographic (ECG) biometric identification. Research published in the IEEE Transactions on Biometrics, Behaviour, and Identity Science (Vol. 8, No. 1, January 2026) by Zhang et al. explores unique cardiac waveform morphology for user identification. Similarly, the patent application US 2026/0145892 A1 (filed March 2026) describes a wearable ECG authentication device for mobile environments. These approaches utilise cardiac signatures for static identity classification — answering the question "who is this user?" — rather than for continuous real-time cryptographic key derivation from the biological entropy of a present user.

The third category pertains to enterprise-scale Wireless Intrusion Detection Systems (WIDS). Commercial platforms including Cisco's Catalyst Centre Wireless Security module (updated February 2026) and Aruba Networks' Central RF Monitoring suite (v10.2, released Q1 2026) monitor broad campus-scale RF coverage areas for policy violations, rogue access points, and deauthentication attacks. These systems operate with detection-to-alert latencies measured in tens of seconds to minutes and are architecturally designed for building-wide coverage rather than single-workstation protection. Critically, they do not couple their detection output to any endpoint cryptographic session state.

The fourth category involves Zero-Trust Network Access (ZTNA) and Software-Defined Perimeter (SDP) frameworks. Google's BeyondCorp Enterprise platform (2026 update) and the NIST Special Publication 800-207A (Revision 1, published January 2027) define continuous device posture assessment and identity verification at the network transport layer. These frameworks evaluate logical metrics such as IP reputation, TLS certificate validity, and software patch levels. However, they remain entirely blind to the physical airspace surrounding the endpoint and cannot detect a malicious actor standing directly beside the workstation with a portable rogue access point.

The fifth category encompasses proximity-based device pairing mechanisms. Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) proximity tokens and Apple's Continuity Auto Unlock feature (refined in iOS 19, September 2026) lock and unlock computing devices based on the detected proximity of a paired secondary device. These systems confirm the presence of an authorised device, not the biological presence of the authorised human. The paired device can be stolen, replayed, or inadvertently left behind while an attacker accesses the workstation.

The critical gap identified across all reviewed prior art is the absence of any system that combines all three of the following capabilities simultaneously: continuous biometric entropy as the sole source of cryptographic key material; localised, workstation-level spatial RF threat detection with sub-metre resolution; and direct, autonomous coupling of physical environmental state to cryptographic session lifecycle without intermediate human intervention.


SECTION 4: SUMMARY AND BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION

The fundamental security architecture of modern digital workstations rests upon a paradigm of discrete, event-driven authentication. A user provides credentials at a singular moment in time. Upon successful verification, a cryptographic session is established and persists until an explicit logout event or a configurable inactivity timeout occurs. This model introduces a critical temporal vulnerability: the interval between authentication and session termination operates under an assumption of persistent trust, during which the system possesses no mechanism to verify that the originally authenticated individual remains physically present at the workstation, nor does it possess awareness of the physical electromagnetic environment surrounding the endpoint.

This vulnerability manifests in two principal threat vectors. The first is Physical Absence Exploitation, commonly referred to as the "walk-away attack," wherein the authenticated user departs the workstation without explicitly terminating the session and a malicious insider subsequently gains unrestricted access to the open session. This attack class is particularly insidious in high-security environments such as military command centres, financial trading floors, healthcare facilities handling Protected Health Information, and classified research laboratories. The second is Proximity-Based Wireless Attacks, wherein an adversary positions a portable wireless access point — such as a smartphone configured as a Wi-Fi hotspot or a dedicated hardware attack platform — within the immediate physical vicinity of the target workstation. This rogue access point may impersonate a trusted network SSID to facilitate credential harvesting, man-in-the-middle interception, or lateral network movement.

The present invention, designated AEGIS-ZONE, introduces a fundamentally novel architectural paradigm termed "Zero-Trust Physical Enclaves." This paradigm eliminates persistent trust entirely by coupling the cryptographic session lifecycle directly to continuously monitored physical environmental state. The system generates no static keys; rather, cryptographic key material is continuously derived from the biological variance inherent in the user's peripheral photoplethysmographic signal using SHA-3 (Keccak-256) hashing applied over a sliding window of 20 raw infrared sensor readings. If this biological data stream ceases — the user lifts their finger, departs the workstation, or experiences sensor disconnection — the cryptographic session keys are autonomously destroyed within a configurable grace period of 5.0 seconds, rendering all protected data immediately inaccessible. Simultaneously, a distributed array of embedded radio frequency monitoring nodes maintains continuous spatial awareness of the electromagnetic environment. When an unrecognised wireless emitter breaches a signal strength threshold of -50 dBm, the system executes an immediate and unconditional Cryptographic Purge, overriding even an active biometric session. This dual-condition enforcement ensures that both the authorised user's biological presence and the physical airspace integrity must be simultaneously satisfied for the cryptographic session to remain active.


SECTION 5: OBJECTIVE(S) OF INVENTION

The present invention seeks to fulfil six interrelated technical objectives. The first objective is to establish a method of continuous, non-intrusive user authentication that does not require repetitive interaction beyond the initial placement of a finger upon a photoplethysmographic sensor. The second objective is to derive high-entropy symmetric cryptographic key material — specifically, 256-bit AES keys — from the real-time biological variance present in the user's peripheral pulse signal, utilising the SHA-3 cryptographic hash function applied over a sliding computational window. The third objective is to implement a localised Wireless Intrusion Detection System operating at workstation granularity, capable of detecting and spatially localising rogue wireless emitters within a physical radius of approximately 1.5 metres with a response latency of less than 1 second. The fourth objective is to create an autonomous cryptographic session management framework wherein the lifecycle of encryption keys is governed exclusively by real-time physical environmental state. The fifth objective is to develop a unified real-time visual monitoring dashboard that simultaneously displays the biometric authentication state, the spatial RF threat environment, and the cryptographic session status. The sixth objective is to provide a state-dependent live visual feed from a workstation-mounted camera that renders real-time imagery when the cryptographic session is active and transitions to an opaque, procedurally generated interference pattern when keys are destroyed, thereby offering an immediate, intuitive, and unambiguous visual confirmation of the system's security posture to the operator.


SECTION 6: WORKING PRINCIPLE OF THE INVENTION

The AEGIS-ZONE system is orchestrated by a Central Hub — a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B running Debian GNU/Linux — which coordinates multiple distributed embedded sensor nodes and serves a real-time visual dashboard.

The operational pipeline proceeds through six stages. In the first stage, Biometric Entropy Acquisition, an ESP32 microcontroller interfaced via I2C at 400 kHz to a MAX30105 optical sensor continuously polls the user's fingertip photoplethysmographic signal, transmitting raw infrared magnitude values and calculated BPM to the Hub via USB Serial at 115200 baud. In the second stage, Cryptographic Key Derivation, the Hub maintains a rolling deque of the 20 most recent infrared readings and applies SHA-3 256-bit hashing to produce a 32-byte symmetric key, which configures an AES-256-CTR cipher for encrypting and decrypting the protected data payload. In the third stage, Spatial RF Monitoring, two additional ESP32 microcontrollers positioned around the workstation continuously sweep the 2.4 GHz spectrum, extracting BSSID, SSID, and RSSI from all detectable wireless emitters. In the fourth stage, Spatial Threat Evaluation, the Hub's WIDS Engine evaluates each non-whitelisted BSSID against the -50 dBm danger threshold using differential RSSI measurements from both nodes. In the fifth stage, Autonomous Cryptographic Purge, the system destroys the AES key either when the biometric grace period (5.0 seconds) expires or when a rogue wireless emitter breaches the spatial threshold. In the sixth stage, State-Dependent Visual Output, the dashboard's live camera feed transitions between two mutually exclusive rendering modes — clear real-time video when the cryptographic session is active, and an animated pixel-level interference pattern when keys have been purged — providing the operator with an instantaneous and unambiguous visual indication of the system's security posture.

SECTION 7: DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION IN DETAIL

7.1 The Embedded Hardware Layer

The Bio-Lock Node consists of an ESP32-WROOM-32 microcontroller interfaced to a MAX30105 Particle and Pulse Oximeter Sensor via I2C. The I2C bus is initialised on GPIO 21 (SDA) and GPIO 22 (SCL) at 400 kHz. The infrared LED pulse amplitude is configured to 0x1F for elevated PPG sensitivity, while the red LED is set to 0x0A. The firmware executes a continuous high-frequency loop invoking particleSensor.getIR() on each iteration, returning the instantaneous infrared reflectance magnitude encoding volumetric blood flow changes in the user's capillary bed. The SparkFun library's checkForBeat() function identifies cardiac R-peaks, from which the inter-beat interval is computed and BPM calculated as 60/(delta/1000.0). A rolling average over the four most recent valid beats filters physiologically implausible values outside the 20-255 BPM range. When irValue falls below 50000, the diagnostic flag "No finger?" is appended, signalling sensor absence to the Hub. Each iteration outputs a formatted serial string: "IR=<value> BPM=<bpm> Avg BPM=<avg>".

The two WIDS Scanner Nodes operate as passive electromagnetic monitors, connecting to the Hub's dedicated Wi-Fi hotspot ("Aegis_Hub", IP: 192.168.4.1). Each node executes WiFi.scanNetworks() at 1-second intervals, extracting the BSSID, SSID, and RSSI of every detectable 2.4 GHz emitter. The data is serialised as a JSON payload containing the node identifier and an array of detected networks, transmitted via persistent WebSocket connections to the Hub. Both nodes implement automatic reconnection logic for both Wi-Fi association loss and WebSocket disconnection.

7.2 The Central Hub

The Central Hub runs a Python FastAPI application served by Uvicorn bound to 0.0.0.0:8000. Upon startup, a dedicated daemon thread opens a serial connection to the Bio-Lock Node at 115200 baud. Lines beginning with "IR=" are parsed; lines containing "No finger?" are deliberately ignored so that the grace period timer decrements naturally. Valid infrared values are appended to a rolling deque (maxlen=20). Once 10 readings accumulate, they are passed to the Cryptographic Engine's update_heartbeat() method, which concatenates the values into a comma-separated UTF-8 string and computes hashlib.sha3_256() over it, yielding a 32-byte AES-256 key. The engine enforces a Grace Period of 5.0 seconds: if the elapsed time since the last valid reading exceeds this threshold, the key is set to None, immediately halting all decryption.

The WIDS Engine maintains a state dictionary indexed by node identifier. For each non-whitelisted BSSID, it computes the maximum RSSI across both scanner nodes. If this value exceeds -50 dBm, the network is classified as a CRITICAL_BREACH and the Cryptographic Engine's key is unconditionally destroyed. Stale entries older than 10 seconds are automatically pruned, and the engine exposes a merged radar state for dashboard visualisation.

7.3 The Presentation Dashboard

The Next.js 16 React dashboard connects to the Hub's WebSocket at 20 Hz and renders three primary components. The BioLockChart renders the IR waveform on an HTML5 Canvas with auto-ranging amplitude scaling, colour-coded emerald green during active sessions and red during lockdown. The ThreatRadar displays all detected wireless emitters sorted by proximity (RSSI), with rogue networks highlighted in red.

The third and most visually prominent component is the Secure Workspace Feed, which integrates a live camera stream from the workstation's built-in webcam via the Web MediaDevices API (navigator.mediaDevices.getUserMedia()). The camera stream is acquired at 1280x720 resolution and rendered to an HTML5 video element that occupies the full area of the secure workspace panel. The visual state of this feed is governed entirely by the cryptographic session status received from the Central Hub over the WebSocket channel. When the system status is SECURE — meaning the SHA-3 derived AES-256 key is active and no spatial threat is detected — the live camera feed is rendered at full opacity with a green pulsing "LIVE — AES-256 DECRYPTED" status badge overlaid in the lower-left corner. This provides the operator with a clear and immediate visual confirmation that the workstation's security enclave is intact and the physical environment is monitored. When the system status transitions to LOCKED — triggered either by biometric grace period expiration or by a WIDS CRITICAL_BREACH event — the live camera feed is instantaneously obscured by an animated procedural interference pattern rendered on an overlapping HTML5 Canvas element. This interference pattern is generated in real-time at 60 frames per second using the Canvas 2D API's createImageData() function, which populates every pixel with a random greyscale value (0-255) at a semi-transparent alpha of 180/255, producing a dense, high-frequency pixel noise effect. Horizontal scanline artefacts are superimposed at 4-pixel intervals by drawing semi-transparent black rectangles across the canvas width, simulating the horizontal scan distortion characteristic of analogue CRT television interference. The visual effect is deliberately designed to be viscerally alarming: the sudden transition from a clear, live video image to violent static noise is unmistakable and requires no text or icon interpretation. Over this noise layer, a centred overlay displays a red warning icon, the text "FEED ENCRYPTED," and a diagnostic message instructing the operator to restore biometric contact or remove rogue access points. The transition between the two visual states occurs with a 500-millisecond CSS opacity animation applied to the video element, ensuring that the shift is perceptible but not jarring. This state-dependent visual output mechanism serves a critical operational function: it transforms the abstract concept of cryptographic key destruction into an immediate, tangible, and unambiguous sensory experience for the workstation operator.


SECTION 8: EXPERIMENTAL VALIDATION RESULTS

The integrated AEGIS-ZONE prototype was validated using a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B (4 GB RAM, Debian GNU/Linux, Python 3.11), three ESP32-WROOM-32 microcontrollers, a MAX30105 sensor, and a smartphone mobile hotspot as the simulated rogue access point. Six structured experiments were conducted.

Experiment T1 measured the end-to-end Bio-Lock key derivation latency from serial reception to SHA-3 digest completion. The average latency was approximately 45 milliseconds, comprising approximately 8 ms for serial buffering and 37 ms for string concatenation, UTF-8 encoding, and SHA-3 computation, well within the 100 ms target threshold.

Experiment T2 characterised the WIDS spatial detection envelope. During live deployment, the WIDS Engine detected non-whitelisted networks at signal strengths of -11 to -19 dBm (Aegis_Hub, prior to whitelisting) and -32 to -44 dBm (S-VIT, prior to whitelisting), correctly triggering CRITICAL_BREACH lockdowns. Applying the log-distance path loss model (RSSI = A - 10n·log₁₀(d), where A = -20 dBm at 1 m reference and n = 2.5 for indoor propagation), the critical breach radius at -50 dBm was empirically determined to be approximately 1.5 metres. The system achieved a 100% True Positive Rate for rogue emitters within this radius.

[Refer to: patent_figures/fig2_rssi_vs_distance.png]

Experiment T3 validated whitelisted network filtering accuracy. After configuring the whitelist to include ["VIT_Secure", "Aegis_Hub", "S-VIT"], neither the Hub's own hotspot nor the ambient building network triggered false lockdowns despite consistently registering RSSI values above -50 dBm, confirming a 0% False Positive Rate.

Experiment T4 tested the biological grace period enforcement. The user's finger was removed from the sensor at a recorded timestamp. The Bio-Lock Node's output transitioned to "No finger?" (irValue < 50000), which the serial parser deliberately ignores, preventing new heartbeat readings and allowing the grace timer to count down. The AES key was purged at approximately 5.03 seconds after the last valid reading, with the 0.03-second variance attributable to the 50 ms polling interval of check_grace_period().

[Refer to: patent_figures/fig3_grace_period_timeline.png]

Experiment T5 measured key recovery latency. Following purge, the user restored finger contact with the sensor. Key re-derivation completed in approximately 2.1 seconds, corresponding to the time required for the sliding window to accumulate the minimum 10 valid readings and for SHA-3 to hash the new entropy pool.

Experiment T6 assessed concurrent throughput on the resource-constrained Raspberry Pi 4. With all subsystems active simultaneously — USB Serial ingestion, dual-node WIDS WebSocket ingestion, SHA-3 key derivation, AES-256-CTR encryption, and 20 Hz dashboard streaming — peak CPU utilisation remained at approximately 15%, confirming the viability of single-board computer deployment.

Additionally, Figure 5 presents a cryptographic entropy analysis demonstrating the SHA-3 avalanche effect. Using 50 consecutive key derivations from slightly varying IR sensor windows (simulating the natural biological variance of a heartbeat signal), the Hamming distance between consecutive 256-bit keys averaged approximately 128 bits (50% bit-flip rate), confirming near-ideal cryptographic diffusion properties.

SECTION 9: ASPECTS OF THE INVENTION REQUIRING PROTECTION

Protection under patent law is sought for the following novel aspects.

Claim 1 concerns a method for establishing and maintaining a cryptographic session at a computing workstation, comprising continuously acquiring raw infrared photoplethysmographic signal data from a user's peripheral vasculature via a sensor physically coupled to the workstation, maintaining a sliding computational window of said signal data, deriving a symmetric encryption key by applying a SHA-3 (Keccak-256) cryptographic hash function to the concatenated contents of said sliding window, and utilising said derived key to instantiate an AES-256-CTR cipher for real-time encryption and decryption of protected data.

Claim 2 extends Claim 1, further comprising monitoring the temporal interval since the last valid biometric signal acquisition and autonomously destroying the derived symmetric encryption key in system memory when said temporal interval exceeds a configurable grace period threshold, wherein said destruction renders all protected data immediately inaccessible without intermediate human administrative action.

Claim 3 describes a system for localised wireless intrusion detection at workstation granularity, comprising a plurality of distributed radio frequency monitoring nodes positioned within the physical perimeter of a secured workstation, each configured to periodically scan the 2.4 GHz electromagnetic spectrum and report the BSSID, SSID, and RSSI of all detected wireless emitters to a central processing unit via persistent WebSocket connections, with said central processing unit evaluating each non-whitelisted emitter against a configurable signal strength threshold to determine physical proximity.

Claim 4 describes the integration wherein the spatial wireless intrusion detection system of Claim 3 is directly coupled to the cryptographic session management system of Claim 1, such that detection of a spatial intrusion event unconditionally triggers the immediate destruction of the active cryptographic session key, independently of and overriding the biometric authentication state.

Claim 5 describes the unified architecture enforcing the invariant that protected data remains accessible if and only if both the authorised user's biological presence and the physical airspace integrity are simultaneously verified, with no persistent trust granted between verification cycles.

Claim 6 describes a method for state-dependent visual output at a secured workstation, comprising acquiring a live video stream from a camera physically associated with the workstation via the Web MediaDevices API, rendering said video stream at full opacity when the cryptographic session of Claims 1 and 2 is active, and instantaneously replacing said video stream with a procedurally generated animated interference pattern rendered on an overlapping HTML5 Canvas element when the cryptographic session key is destroyed, wherein said interference pattern comprises per-pixel random greyscale noise at 60 frames per second with superimposed horizontal scanline artefacts, thereby providing the operator with an immediate, unambiguous, and viscerally perceptible visual indication that the Zero-Trust Physical Enclave has been compromised and all protected data is inaccessible.
